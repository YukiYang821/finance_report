Complete Mathematical Formulation: From Dynamic Portfolio Optimization to Quantum-Solvable Models

1.1 Dynamic Portfolio Optimization: A Multi-Period Investment Challenge

Investment Context

In the landscape of asset management, Dynamic Portfolio Optimization (DPO) involves a collection of heterogeneous assets—including equities, fixed-income securities, alternative investments, and more—with the objective of identifying optimal trading actions across a sequence of rebalancing timestamps. The core mission is to delineate the most advantageous trading trajectory over a defined time period.

Primary Financial Objectives:
  • Maximize Expected Returns: Generate the highest possible profit from the investment portfolio
  • Minimize Portfolio Risk: Reduce volatility and potential losses through diversification
  • Control Transaction Costs: Manage the expenses associated with buying and selling assets
  • Satisfy Budget Constraints: Operate within specified investment limits and allocations
Temporal Dimension Challenges:
Multiple Time Periods

Investment decisions span \(N_t\) discrete time points with regular rebalancing intervals (e.g., monthly)

Dynamic Adjustments

Portfolio weights are adjusted at each rebalancing point based on market conditions

Inter-temporal Trade-offs

Decisions at time \(t\) affect both immediate returns and future transaction costs

1.2 Mathematical Formulation: Building the Objective Function

1

Core Objective Function Definition

The complete optimization problem is defined by maximizing the following objective function:

\[ O(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = F(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) - \frac{\gamma}{2}R(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) - C(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) \]
2

Expected Return Component

\[ F(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} \omega_{t,a} \mu_{t,a} \]

where \(\mu_{t,a} = \log\left(\frac{P_{t+1,a}}{P_{t,a}}\right)\) is the logarithmic return of asset \(a\) at time \(t\), calculated from price changes.

3

Portfolio Risk Component

\[ R(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \boldsymbol{\omega}_t^T \Sigma_t \boldsymbol{\omega}_t \]

where \(\Sigma_t\) is the \(N_a \times N_a\) covariance matrix of logarithmic returns at time \(t\). The risk aversion coefficient \(\gamma\) ensures comparable magnitude between return and risk.

Risk Modeling Alternatives:
  • Basic Covariance: Traditional Markowitz approach
  • Semi-Covariance: Penalizes only downside risk
  • Shrinkage Covariance: Enhanced robustness to sample volatility
4

Transaction Costs Component

\[ C(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = \nu \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} |\omega_{t,a} - \omega_{t-1,a}| \]

where \(\nu \) is transaction fee rate, \(\omega_{-1,a} = 0\) is initial condition.

5

Portfolio Constraints

Budget Constraint
\[ \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} \omega_{t,a} = K \quad \forall t \]

Total investment across all assets must equal the budget \(K\) at each time period.

Individual Asset Limits
\[ \omega_{t,a} \leq K' \quad \forall t,a \]

Maximum investment in any single asset is limited to \(K'\).

Non-negativity Constraint
\[ \omega_{t,a} \geq 0 \quad \forall t,a \]

Short selling is not permitted in this formulation.

1.3 QUBO Transformation: Preparing for Quantum Solvers

1

Normalization and Variable Redefinition

To simplify constraints, we normalize all investments by the total budget \(K\):

\[ \tilde{\omega}_{t,a} = \frac{\omega_{t,a}}{K}, \quad \tilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}} = \frac{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}{K} \]

The budget constraint becomes:

\[ \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} \tilde{\omega}_{t,a} = 1 \quad \forall t \]

And individual limits transform to \(\tilde{\omega}_{t,a} \leq K'/K\).

2

Quadratic Approximation of Transaction Costs

The absolute value in transaction costs is neither quadratic nor linear. We approximate it quadratically:

\[ C(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}) = \nu\lambda \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} (\tilde{\omega}_{t,a} - \tilde{\omega}_{t-1,a})^2 \]

where \(\lambda\) is a parameter introduced to adjust the quadratic difference between weights at different times. This transformation preserves the essential properties while enabling QUBO formulation.

3

Constraint Penalization

Hard constraints are converted to soft constraints using penalty terms:

\[ B(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}) = \rho \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \left( \sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1} \tilde{\omega}_{t,a} - 1 \right)^2 \]

with Lagrange multiplier \(\rho\) balancing constraint enforcement and original objective optimization.

4

Binary Variable Encoding

Continuous investments are discretized using binary variables:

\[ \tilde{\omega}_{t,a} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{r=0}^{N_r-1} 2^r x_{t,a,r} \]

where \(x_{t,a,r} \in \{0,1\}\) are binary decision variables. Each asset at each time step is represented by \(N_r\) bits (typically \(N_r = 4\), giving 16 precision levels).

Variable Count Analysis:
Parameter Symbol Typical Values
Number of time steps\(N_t\)2-22
Number of assets\(N_a\)3-7
Binary precision bits\(N_r\)1-4
Total binary variables\(N_q = N_t \times N_a \times N_r\)6-112
5

Complete QUBO Formulation

The complete QUBO objective function to be minimized is:

\[ Q = \sum_{t=0}^{N_t-1} \left[ -\boldsymbol{\mu}_t^T\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_t + \frac{\gamma}{2}\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_t^T\Sigma_t\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_t + \nu\lambda(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_t - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_{t-1})^2 + \rho\left(\sum_{a=0}^{N_a-1}\tilde{\omega}_{t,a} - 1\right)^2 \right] \]

In standard QUBO form:

\[ Q = \sum_{i,j} Q_{ij} x_i x_j \]

where \(Q\) is a symmetric \(N_q \times N_q\) matrix containing all linear and quadratic terms.

1.4 Ising Model Mapping: Quantum-Ready Formulation

1

Binary to Spin Transformation

The final step converts the QUBO problem to an Ising Hamiltonian suitable for quantum computation:

Variable transformation:
\[ x_i = \frac{1 - Z_i}{2} \]
Resulting Ising Hamiltonian:
\[ H = \sum_{i=1}^{N_q} h_i Z_i + \sum_{i< j} J_{ij} Z_i Z_j \]
Local fields \(h_i\)
\[ h_i = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_j Q_{ij} \]
Coupling strengths \(J_{ij}\)
\[ J_{ij} = \frac{1}{4} Q_{ij} \]
2

Qubit Mapping Strategy

Binary variables are mapped to physical qubits using the indexing scheme:

\[ q = r + N_r \times a + t \times (N_a \times N_r) \]

where \(q \in [0, N_q-1]\) is the qubit index, \(r\) is the precision bit index, \(a\) is the asset index, and \(t\) is the time index.

Example: XXL Problem Size

For \(N_t=4\), \(N_a=7\), \(N_r=4\):

  • Total qubits: \(N_q = 4 \times 7 \times 4 = 112\)
  • Each time step: 28 qubits (7 assets × 4 bits)
  • Precision: 16 investment levels per asset
  • Problem scales polynomially with \(N_t\), \(N_a\), \(N_r\)

1.5 Mathematical Formulation Summary

Complete Transformation Pathway

①
Financial Problem

Dynamic Portfolio Optimization with \(N_a\) assets over \(N_t\) time periods

②
Optimization

Maximize \(O(\boldsymbol{\Omega})\) \(= F(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) - \frac{\gamma}{2}R(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) - C(\boldsymbol{\Omega})\) subject to constraints

③
QUBO Formulation

Discretized to \(N_q\) binary variables: \(Q = \sum_{i,j} Q_{ij} x_i x_j\)

④
Ising Hamiltonian

Quantum-ready form: \(H = \sum_i h_i Z_i + \sum_{i< j} J_{ij} Z_i Z_j\)

Key Mathematical Insights

  • The transformation preserves the essential financial semantics while enabling quantum computation
  • Problem complexity grows as \(O(N_t \times N_a \times N_r)\), requiring scalable quantum approaches
  • The block-diagonal matrix structure with weak inter-block coupling informs algorithmic design
  • Precision requirements (e.g., int8 for CIM) necessitate careful numerical considerations

Ready for Quantum Solutions: The mathematical formulation is now complete. The Ising Hamiltonian \(H\) serves as the universal interface for quantum solvers. In the next section, we explore two distinct quantum computing approaches to find the ground state of this Hamiltonian.

Part 2 Mathematical Problem → Quantum Solutions

Two Distinct Hardware Approaches for Solving the Ising Model

2.1 Superconducting Gate-Based Quantum Computer (VQE Method)

Based on: Scaling the Variational Quantum Eigensolver for Dynamic Portfolio Optimization

2.1.1 Algorithm Core: Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)

VQE is a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that finds the ground state (lowest energy) of a Hamiltonian by minimizing the expectation value through iterative optimization of a parameterized quantum circuit.

Complete VQE Workflow:
1
Problem Encoding

Map the Ising Hamiltonian \(H\) to the quantum processor. Each spin variable \(Z_i\) corresponds to a physical qubit.

\[ H = \sum_i h_i Z_i + \sum_{i< j} J_{ij} Z_i Z_j \]
2
Ansatz Design

Construct a parameterized quantum circuit \(U(\theta)\) that can approximate the ground state. We investigated four ansatz types:

Cyclic Ansatz: Reference implementation from literature, high depth (2347 for 112 qubits)
Real Amplitudes (RA): 3 layers, real-valued amplitudes, depth 358 for 112 qubits
Optimized RA: Reduced depth (100 for 112 qubits) but increased parameters (784 vs 448)
Tailored Ansatz: Custom design, depth 72 for 112 qubits, best performance
3
Quantum Execution

Execute the circuit on IBM Torino QPU and measure the expectation value:

\[ \langle H \rangle_\theta = \langle 0|U^\dagger(\theta) H U(\theta)|0 \rangle \]

Using IBM's estimator primitive with 25,000 shots for statistical accuracy.

4
Classical Optimization

Update parameters \(\theta\) using classical optimization to minimize \(\langle H \rangle\):

Conjugate Gradient (CG): Gradient-based, reference implementation, struggles with noise

Differential Evolution (DE): Gradient-free, population-based, robust against quantum noise and barren plateaus

DE Hyperparameters:
  • Strategy: 'best2bin'
  • Mutation range: (0, 0.25)
  • Recombination: 0.4
  • Population: 92 for XXL size
  • Generations: 40 for XXL size
5
Convergence & Sampling

Iterate steps 3-4 until convergence (cost change < 2.5%). Sample the final circuit to obtain portfolio solutions.

2.1.2 Hardware Implementation: IBM Torino Quantum Processor

Technical Specifications:
Qubit Architecture

133 superconducting transmon qubits arranged in a hexagonal lattice with heavy-hex connectivity

Operating Conditions

Ultra-low temperature: ~15 millikelvin in dilution refrigerator

Gate Performance

Average gate fidelity: 99.9%, Coherence times: T₁ ~ 100μs, T₂ ~ 150μs

Native Gate Set

√X, RZ, ECR (echoed cross-resonance) as native two-qubit gate

Technical Challenges Overcome:
Barren Plateaus

Gradient-based optimizers (CG) failed due to exponentially vanishing gradients. DE's gradient-free approach avoided this issue.

Quantum Noise

Raw execution without error mitigation required robust ansatz design and optimization strategy.

Limited Connectivity

Hexagonal lattice connectivity required SWAP gates for long-range interactions, increasing circuit depth.

2.2 Coherent Ising Machine (CIM) – Optical Quantum Annealer

Based on: Note on QUBO Quantum Solver – Utilizing Block Coordinate Descent for Hardware Constraints

2.2.1 Physical Principle: Optical Parametric Oscillator Network

Core Physical Mechanism:

A Coherent Ising Machine (CIM) is an optical quantum computing device that implements the Ising model using a network of Degenerate Optical Parametric Oscillators (DOPOs). Each DOPO pulse represents an Ising spin, with its phase (0 or π) corresponding to spin orientation (±1).

Phase Synchronization

Optical pulses naturally synchronize to minimize system energy

All-to-All Connectivity

Optical interference naturally implements full coupling matrix

Nonlinear Dynamics

Optical nonlinearity drives system toward ground state

CIM Hardware Advantages:

Room Temperature Operation

No cryogenic requirements unlike superconducting qubits

Millisecond Timescales

Optical processing enables rapid convergence

Natural Scalability

Optical systems can scale to 10,000+ spins

Energy Efficiency

Significantly lower power consumption than superconducting systems

2.2.2 Core Innovation: Block Coordinate Descent (BCD)

1

Problem Analysis & Motivation

1
Dimensionality Explosion

Global QUBO dimension: \(N_q = N_t \times N_a \times N_r\) grows rapidly

\[ \text{Complexity} = O(2^{N_t \times N_a \times N_r}) \]
2
Hardware Precision Constraint

QBoson CIM requires int8 coefficients [-128, 127]. Direct quantization loses weak couplings.

3
Matrix Structure

QUBO matrix has strong intra-block diagonal terms and weak inter-block off-diagonal terms.

2

BCD Solution Design

Variable Partitioning

Partition global variables into \(m\) temporal blocks along time dimension:

\[ \mathcal{V} = \{\mathcal{V}_1, \mathcal{V}_2, \ldots, \mathcal{V}_m\} \]

Each block \(\mathcal{V}_i\) contains all assets at time step \(i\).

Local Subproblem Construction

For target block \(\mathcal{V}_i\), construct local QUBO matrix considering only adjacent blocks:

\[ Q_{\text{sub}} = \begin{bmatrix} Q_{i-1} & Q_{c,(i-1,i)} & 0 \\ Q_{c,(i-1,i)}^T & Q_i & Q_{c,(i,i+1)} \\ 0 & Q_{c,(i,i+1)}^T & Q_{i+1} \end{bmatrix} \]
Precision Adaptation

Apply optimal-preserving coefficient compression to reduce dynamic range while preserving global minimizer.

Dynamic Range Reduction: Iteratively adjust extreme values
Quantization: \(X_{\text{int8}} = \text{clip}(\text{round}(\frac{127}{\alpha(X)} \cdot X), -128, 127)\)
Iterative Optimization

Optimize each block sequentially while fixing others, iterate until convergence:

\[ V_i^* = \arg\min_{V_i \in \{0,1\}^{n_i}} V_i^T \hat{Q}_i V_i \]

where \(\hat{Q}_i = Q_i + \text{diag}(2V_{i-1}Q_{c,(i-1,i)} + 2V_{i+1}Q_{c,(i,i+1)})\).

3

BCD Benefits

Complexity Reduction

From \(O(2^{N_t \times N_a \times N_r})\) to \(O(N_t \times 2^{N_a \times N_r})\)

Preserves Weak Couplings

Transaction cost terms between adjacent time steps are retained

Hardware Compatibility

Enables int8 quantization without information loss

Risk Model Analysis:

Three covariance methods were tested with CIM:

Basic Covariance

Traditional Markowitz approach, penalizes both upside and downside volatility. Provides balanced risk assessment but may be too conservative.

Semi-Covariance

Only penalizes downside risk, better aligns with investor preferences who care more about losses than gains. Captures asymmetry in return distributions.

Shrinkage Covariance

Enhanced robustness to sample volatility through regularization. Combines sample covariance with structured estimator to reduce estimation error.

Key Finding: Semi-covariance consistently performed best across all solvers and problem sizes, demonstrating superior alignment with financial objectives.

Limitations & Future Work:
Current Limitation

BCD only scales with time steps \(N_t\), not with assets \(N_a\) within same time step. Large asset dimensions still pose computational challenges.

Future Direction 1

Extend BCD framework to asset dimension for larger \(N_a\). Develop hierarchical decomposition strategies combining both temporal and asset dimensions.

Future Direction 2

Test BCD on superconducting quantum processors with QAOA. Explore hybrid quantum-classical approaches combining VQE and BCD methodologies.

Future Direction 3

Integrate with error correction for improved solution quality. Develop fault-tolerant versions of BCD for future quantum hardware generations.

Application Scenario Analysis

Superconducting QC Suitable Scenarios

  • Parameterized optimization: Medium-scale problems requiring fine-tuning
  • Dynamic portfolio optimization: Multi-period adjustment of small-medium portfolios
  • Algorithm research: Quantum algorithm development and validation
  • Educational demonstrations: Quantum computing teaching and concept proof

CIM Suitable Scenarios

  • Large-scale combinatorial optimization: Hundreds to thousands of variables
  • Real-time financial decisions: Millisecond response requirements
  • High-frequency trading: Fast solving for trading strategy optimization
  • Risk management: Large-scale risk analysis and stress testing

Research Conclusions & Future Outlook

Quantum Advantage Demonstrated

Both approaches show practical quantum utility: VQE achieves near-classical accuracy on 112-qubit problems, while CIM demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed advantage.

Complementary Technical Paths

Gate-based superconducting and annealing-based optical approaches target different problem scales and applications, together expanding quantum finance capabilities.

Practical Financial Applications

Quantum computing shows promising applications in portfolio optimization, risk management, derivatives pricing, especially for complex nonlinear problems.

Future Research Directions

Further work needed in error mitigation, algorithm optimization, hardware stability, and exploring hybrid quantum-classical approaches for real-world deployment.
